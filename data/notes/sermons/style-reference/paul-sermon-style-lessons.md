# Paul Sermon Style Lessons

Learned from Paul's direct edits to the working copy of "A Friend In High Places" on 2026-03-18.

## Formatting lessons
- Title should be centered and strong, but not overworked.
- Scripture blocks should remain in red italics.
- Big points should be bold.
- Only true sub-point lines should be underlined.
- Minor points should preserve the numbering hierarchy clearly:
  - a)
  - i)
  - 1)
  - a)
- Do not flatten the point hierarchy.

## Flow / tone lessons
- Paul's sermon flow is less stiff and more natural than a formal outline dump.
- Keep the rhythm closer to how Paul already starts a sermon draft.
- Use shorter, cleaner thought units.
- Preserve punch and readability over overly polished prose.
- Do not over-format or over-emphasize too many lines.
- Paul often keeps strong explanatory lines plain instead of over-styling them.
- The sermon should feel preachable, not just organized.
- Spacing and hierarchy should help him preach, not just help someone read.

## Additional formatting lessons from later edits
- Keep the title centered, but simple.
- Scripture references with full text should sit naturally under the relevant point, not feel mechanically pasted in.
- Numbering rhythm matters a lot; flattening it makes the sermon feel wrong fast.
- Underlining should be selective and sparse.
- Big points should stand out clearly, but too much emphasis makes the page feel noisy.

## Content handling lessons
- Content can come from the source sermon, but formatting and movement must feel like Paul's sermon style.
- If Paul has already started a draft, use his in-progress version as the primary style anchor.
- When source scriptures are referenced in the sermon, write out the full scripture text under the relevant point.

## Guardrail
When adapting another preacher's sermon into Paul's style:
- use their content only
- use Paul's structure, hierarchy, and sermon feel
- prefer Paul's existing draft rhythm over a generic outline style

## Lessons from Paul's finished version of "A Friend In High Places"
- Paul keeps the original gist and revelation, but trims and reshapes the language so it sounds naturally preachable in his own voice.
- He preserves the source preacher's point progression without sounding like he copied a manuscript mechanically.
- He simplifies sentences and favors momentum over over-explaining.
- He keeps the hierarchy visible and preachable: big points, sub-points, minor points, and supporting scripture all working together.
- He uses scripture under the point as support, not as a clunky inserted block.
- He keeps the page useful for preaching, not just study.
- He is willing to remove extra stiffness, extra wording, or extra formatting even if the original source included it.
- The goal is not a transcript transfer; it is a faithful translation into Paul's sermon format and voice while keeping the same revelation flow.
