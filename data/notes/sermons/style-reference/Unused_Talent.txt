Unused Talent
Intro: In the late 1800s there was a man named Vincent van Gogh. Today he is considered one of the greatest painters in history. But what most people don’t realize is that for most of his life, he didn’t use his talent at all. Van Gogh tried several different careers. He worked in an art dealership. He tried teaching. He even tried becoming a preacher. But nothing seemed to work. For years he drifted from one thing to another, discouraged and uncertain about his purpose. It wasn’t until he was 27 years old that he finally began painting seriously. And when he did, the talent that had been sitting unused suddenly came alive. In just ten years, he produced over 2,000 works of art—paintings that today hang in the greatest museums in the world. But imagine if he had never picked up the brush. Imagine if that talent had remained buried. The world would have lost something remarkable simply because a gift was never used.
- Today, going read very familiar SCR that tells about a master giving his servants “talents” to steward
- Some did well, and made impact / some wasted their talent and made no impact

Story: In 1905, a miner in South Africa named Frederick Wells was doing a routine inspection of the mine wall when something caught his eye. About nine meters up the wall, he saw a small glimmer. At first he thought it was just a piece of glass reflecting the light. But he climbed up, pulled out his knife, and pried it loose. What he held in his hand turned out to be the largest diamond ever discovered. It was called the Cullinan Diamond. Before it was cut, it weighed over 3,100 carats—more than a pound of solid diamond. But here’s the interesting part. For who knows how long, that diamond had been sitting buried in the rock, completely unnoticed. Workers had passed that spot countless times. People had walked by it every day without realizing what was there. But once it was discovered and brought out, it was cut into several diamonds that today sit in the British Crown Jewels.
         1) The tragedy is not that people don’t use talent - it’s that they don’t even realize it’s there
            a) You have abilities you don’t even realize that no one else has - but have to use / develop them
      i) Some actively use their talent for bad: 
         a) We see corrupt politicians / secular artists / wall street - using their god-given talent for personal gain
      ii) Others just never use their talent at all: 
         1) Seen people w/ incredible talent never use it for something beyond themselves
            a) And they have a huge diamond hidden in themselves, waiting for them to use it

Story: In the 1800s there was a Sunday School teacher in Chicago named Edward Kimball. Kimball wasn’t a famous preacher. He wasn’t a great theologian. He was just an ordinary man who loved God and wanted to help people. One day he felt convicted that he needed to talk to one of the young men in his class about his salvation. The young man worked at a shoe store. Kimball admitted later that he was nervous the whole way there. He almost turned around several times because he didn’t feel qualified. But finally he walked into that store, found the young man in the back stocking shoes, and simply shared the gospel with him. That young man gave his life to Christ. His name was Dwight L. Moody. Moody later became one of the greatest evangelists in history. Through his ministry thousands of people came to Christ. One of those men influenced another evangelist named Billy Sunday, whose ministry influenced Mordecai Ham, who later preached a revival where a young man named Billy Graham was saved. And Billy Graham went on to preach the gospel to millions of people around the world. But all traces back to one guy, who didn’t think he had any talent, but simple used what God gave him - and millions have been saved as a result.

Closing Ill: In 1912, the Titanic sank in the North Atlantic after hitting an iceberg. Over 1,500 people died in that disaster. But when investigators studied what happened, they discovered something troubling. The ship actually had enough lifeboats to save far more people than were rescued. Yet many lifeboats were lowered into the water only half full. One lifeboat that could hold 65 people left the ship with only 28 people inside. Another left with 12 people.Out of the fear of uncertainty, many passengers simply stayed where they were. They hesitated. They doubted. They waited too long.
